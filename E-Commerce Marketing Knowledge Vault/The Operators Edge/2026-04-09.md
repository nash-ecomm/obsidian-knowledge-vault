## Deep Dive: Shopify Functions — The June 30 Migration You Cannot Ignore

Shopify Scripts are dead. [The official deprecation notice](https://changelog.shopify.com/posts/shopify-scripts-deprecation) is unambiguous: on June 30, 2026, all Scripts stop executing — permanently, for all stores, with no opt-out or extension path. If your store uses the Script Editor today to power custom discount logic (tiered pricing, BOGO, bundle deals), shipping rate rules, or payment method filtering, those behaviors will break on July 1 with zero warning to customers. The checkout simply won't apply the logic.

The replacement is [Shopify Functions](https://shopify.dev/docs/apps/build/functions/migrating-from-shopify-scripts) — compiled WebAssembly modules that execute within Shopify's own infrastructure. The migration isn't just a format change; it's an architectural upgrade. Scripts ran on a legacy interpreter with significant limitations. Functions run in **sub-5ms** with no cold starts, execute consistently regardless of storefront stack (Liquid, Hydrogen, headless), and have access to the full extensibility API. Critically, Scripts and Functions can run simultaneously in the same store during your migration window — so you can build, test, and validate your Function logic without disabling Scripts first.

The mapping is direct: **Line item Scripts** → Discount Functions (product and order discounts); **Shipping Scripts** → Delivery Customization Functions; **Payment Scripts** → Payment Customization Functions. The [Shopify Help Center transition guide](https://help.shopify.com/en/manual/checkout-settings/script-editor/transitioning-to-functions) walks through each Script type with its corresponding Function API. For non-technical operators, the practical path is checking whether your app stack (Rebuy, Carthook, Bold, etc.) has already migrated — [Rebuy, for example, published its own deprecation prep guide](https://help.rebuyengine.com/en/articles/10732595-preparing-for-shopify-scripts-discounting-deprecation) with migration steps for merchants relying on its discount logic. If you built custom Scripts in-house, you need a developer on this today.

Functions also unlock what Scripts never could. Beyond replacing existing logic, Functions support **cart transforms** (modify cart line properties before checkout), **checkout validation** (enforce custom business rules before payment), and **order routing** (custom fulfillment logic). For DTC operators running complex promotions, subscription + one-time purchase bundles, or DTC-to-wholesale dual-channel stores, this is genuinely new capability — not just a migration chore.

> [!warning] Action Required Before June 30, 2026
> Check your Script Editor right now: Shopify admin → Settings → Apps and sales channels → Script Editor. If you see active Scripts, open a support ticket with your dev agency this week. Migration involves writing a Function app (or installing a compatible one), testing against your existing Script behavior, and validating edge cases (stacking discounts, B2B pricing rules, etc.). Three months is not a lot of runway if you have complex logic.

> [!info] Scripts → Functions Mapping at a Glance
> | Script Type | Replacement Function API | New Capabilities Added |
> |---|---|---|
> | Line item (discounts) | Discount Function API | Unified product + order + shipping discounts |
> | Shipping scripts | Delivery Customization | Custom carrier logic, order routing |
> | Payment scripts | Payment Customization | Method filtering, conditions, hide/show |
> | *(not in Scripts)* | Cart Transform | Modify cart properties pre-checkout |
> | *(not in Scripts)* | Checkout Validation | Block invalid orders before payment |

## Deep Dive: Shopify Capital — Revenue-Based Financing Without the Bank

For DTC operators, the standard funding gap is painfully familiar: you need inventory 60–90 days before your peak season, your revenue is seasonal or lumpy, and bank loans require 2+ years of audited financials plus personal guarantees most founders won't sign. [Shopify Capital](https://www.shopify.com/capital) is purpose-built for this gap — and the scale of its deployment ($5B+ to merchants since launch) means it's now a first-consideration option, not a last resort.

The mechanics are DTC-native: Shopify's ML underwriting model analyzes your live store data — sales velocity, dispute rate, churn, customer engagement — and makes automatic funding offers without a formal application. Most six-figure US merchants see initial offers of **$10K–$150K**, with brands processing higher volume accessing up to $5M. The cost of capital is a **factor rate of 1.10 or 1.13** (meaning you repay $110K or $113K for every $100K borrowed), and repayment is structured as a **fixed percentage of daily Shopify sales** — it scales with your revenue, so slow weeks don't trigger missed payments. Funds clear in **1–3 business days** from acceptance. [Shopify's merchant financing overview](https://www.shopify.com/blog/merchant-financing) positions it explicitly for inventory purchases, marketing pushes, and seasonal hiring — the three highest-leverage uses in the DTC calendar.

The 36% average sales increase cited across Capital merchant data is worth interrogating. The causality isn't purely financial: operators who accept Capital tend to be growth-oriented and use the funds to buy inventory in bulk (capturing **20–40% cost savings** over just-in-time purchasing), fund a paid media sprint during a peak window, or add SKUs that had been delayed by cash constraints. The real advantage vs. a bank line of credit isn't just speed — it's that repayment is **tied to outcomes**. A bad month where sales drop 30% means your Capital repayment also drops 30% that month. That's structurally impossible with a fixed monthly loan payment.

The constraints are real: Capital requires Shopify Payments as your processor in most markets (US, Canada, UK, Australia currently), and offers are invitation-only — you cannot apply cold. If you don't have an active offer in your admin, the answer is to optimize your store metrics (reduce disputes, increase average order volume, stabilize monthly sales) until the model surfaces one. For brands that do have an offer: compare the factor rate cost against your next best alternative (a 10% annual line of credit costs roughly 2.5% for a 90-day cycle — similar to a 1.10 factor rate on a 90-day inventory play).

> [!tip] Takeaway
> If you have a Shopify Capital offer in your admin and a clear inventory use case (Q4 peak stock, new SKU launch, bulk order at a supplier discount), run the math: factor cost ÷ expected incremental margin from the inventory play. At 1.10 factor rate on a 90-day repayment cycle, you're paying roughly 10% of principal — if the inventory play returns 30%+ margin uplift on that cash, it's accretive.

## Quick Headlines

**Email & SMS Flows vs. Campaigns — The 2026 Klaviyo Benchmark:** Klaviyo's 2026 benchmark report (183,000+ customers) makes the flows-first argument with hard data. [SMS flows are 7.6% of total sends but drive 45.2% of SMS revenue](https://www.klaviyo.com/products/sms-marketing/benchmarks) — an ~8× higher revenue-per-recipient (RPR) than broadcast campaigns. [Email flows tell the same story](https://www.klaviyo.com/products/email-marketing/benchmarks): 5.3% of sends, 41% of email revenue, 18× higher RPR than campaigns. Top 10% email flows hit RPR of **$7.79** with click rates above 10%; top 10% SMS flows clear **$5 RPR**. The implication for operators spending heavily on campaign sends: every hour invested in flow optimization (abandoned cart depth, post-purchase upsell sequencing, winback triggers) has a structurally higher ceiling than any campaign send frequency increase.

## Deep Dive: Checkout Friction — The 14-Decision-Point Audit

Most DTC operators optimize traffic acquisition and PDP copy while leaving checkout UX largely untouched. The data suggests this is exactly backwards. [Baymard Institute's checkout usability research](https://baymard.com/blog/ecommerce-checkout-usability-report) shows **65% of leading ecommerce sites perform "mediocre" or worse** on checkout UX — and the primary culprit is form complexity. The average US checkout presents shoppers with **23.48 form elements**; Baymard's benchmark for optimal flow is **12–14**. The excess fields aren't just inconvenient — they compound cognitive load at the highest-intent moment in the funnel, converting buyers back into browsers.

The single highest-yield checkout fix is guest checkout as the default path. Baymard's data puts **account creation as the trigger for 26% of all checkout abandonments** — a number that has remained stubbornly consistent despite years of UX research proving the fix. The correct architecture: present guest checkout prominently (not buried below "Sign In"), complete the purchase, then offer account creation on the confirmation page where the customer's name, email, and address are already captured — requiring only a password input. Post-purchase account creation converts at materially higher rates than pre-purchase forced registration because the psychological dynamic is inverted: the customer is satisfied, not blocked. [ATTN Agency's checkout psychology guide](https://www.attnagency.com/blog/checkout-flow-psychology-friction-elimination-conversion-optimization-2026) frames the average DTC checkout as **14 discrete decision points**, each one a potential abandonment trigger — and identifies progress indicators (step 1 of 3, step 2 of 3) as reducing mid-checkout dropout by **23%**, simply by eliminating the uncertainty of "how much longer is this?"

On mobile, the form field problem is compounded by keyboard friction. Express payment options — Shop Pay, Apple Pay, Google Pay — bypass the form entirely: [research cited by ATTN Agency](https://www.attnagency.com/blog/checkout-flow-psychology-friction-elimination-conversion-optimization-2026) puts the click reduction at **120 clicks down to 4** for digital wallet users. Brands reporting on Apple Pay adoption see a **+22.3% average conversion lift** purely from the reduced input requirement. The trust signal layer closes the gap: [CXL's original research on checkout trust seals](https://cxl.com/research-study/checkout-optimization/) found that badge placement adjacent to the payment field — not in the footer, not above the fold — drives the highest measurable lift. Recognition rates: McAfee (79%), Verisign (76%), PayPal (72%); PayPal leads on perceived security (63% of respondents). Brands with low name recognition benefit disproportionately from visible trust signals because they're compensating for the trust deficit a recognized brand doesn't have. Taken together, [Growth Engines benchmarks](https://growth-engines.com/insights/ecommerce/ecommerce-checkout-optimization) suggest optimizing checkout friction can recover up to **35% of lost checkout conversions** — making it one of the highest-ROI CRO investments relative to implementation effort.

> [!tip] Takeaway
> Run a checkout audit this week: count your live form fields (most merchants have 20+), confirm guest checkout is the first and largest option presented, and verify your payment trust badge is positioned inline with the payment form — not the footer. Add Shop Pay or Apple Pay if not already live. These four changes require no A/B test infrastructure and can be shipped in a day.

> [!info] Checkout Friction by Abandonment Cause (Baymard 2025)
> | Abandonment Trigger | Share of Abandonments | Fix |
> |---------------------|-----------------------|-----|
> | Unexpected shipping costs | **48%** | Show shipping threshold in cart, pre-checkout |
> | Required account creation | **26%** | Default to guest checkout; offer account post-purchase |
> | Checkout too complex / long | **22%** | Cut to 12–14 form elements; add progress indicator |
> | Security concerns (card data) | **25%** | Trust badge adjacent to payment field (CXL: McAfee, PayPal) |
>

## Deep Dive: Collection Pages — The Forgotten Revenue Layer

If PDP is the closing argument and checkout is the handshake, the collection page is the discovery engine — and most DTC brands have left it almost entirely unoptimized. Collection pages (PLPs) generate **approximately 30% of Shopify organic traffic** while receiving a fraction of the CRO attention that PDPs command. The consequence is predictable: [Baymard's 2025 Product Lists and Filtering benchmark](https://baymard.com/blog/current-state-product-list-and-filtering) finds **58% of desktop ecommerce sites and 78% of mobile sites** score "mediocre or worse" on product list and filtering UX — despite filtering being the primary tool shoppers use to narrow a catalog to relevant options.

The gap between what brands implement and what shoppers need is specific and measurable. Baymard's benchmark of 21,000+ manually reviewed UX parameters finds: **51% of sites do not provide the 5 essential filter types** (price, customer rating, brand, size/variant, and a category-specific attribute), and **69% do not provide the 4 essential sorting options** (Relevance, Best Sellers, Price Low-to-High, Price High-to-Low). Both gaps create the same shopper behavior: unable to narrow results efficiently, visitors either scroll without purpose or exit to a competitor with better discoverability. On mobile — where **67–70% of Shopify traffic arrives** — the problem is acute: mobile CVR averages **1.1–1.8%** against a desktop rate of **3.5–4.5%**, and a large share of that gap is attribution to navigation depth, not creative quality. [Vervaunt's PLP optimization guide](https://vervaunt.com/ecommerce-listings-page-optimisation) recommends tracking filter usage events in GA4 to identify which filter types shoppers actually use in each collection — the data often reveals that 2–3 filters drive 80% of filtering interactions, allowing brands to surface those attributes higher and collapse rarely-used options.

Sort order has an outsized impact that most brands haven't tested. A/B test data consistently finds that defaulting to **"Best Sellers"** outperforms "Newest," "Featured," or alphabetical sort — because social proof operates at the browse layer, not just on the PDP. Displaying star ratings on the product card (not just on the PDP) gives shoppers a trust signal during the scan-and-compare phase, when purchase intent is forming. Color swatches over text labels in filter facets test positively across categories — the visual reference reduces the cognitive step of translating a name ("Dusty Rose") into an actual color. And quick-add-to-cart buttons on product cards — visible on hover for desktop, always-on for mobile — reduce the trip from browse to intent by eliminating the PDP visit for repeat or high-confidence shoppers.

> [!tip] Takeaway
> Audit your top 3 collection pages against Baymard's baseline: do you have all 5 essential filter types live? Are the 4 sort options present with "Best Sellers" as the default? Are star ratings visible on the product card without clicking through? If any are missing, these are same-week additions. Then instrument filter usage in GA4 to see which facets your shoppers actually engage — most brands find their filter taxonomy is built on assumptions, not behavioral data.

> [!warning] The Mobile PLP Gap
> Mobile traffic is ~70% of Shopify sessions but converts at less than half the desktop rate. A significant contributor is PLP navigation: too many taps to reach a relevant product, no sticky filter bar, and product cards too small to trigger confident clicks. Prioritize a sticky filter/sort bar on mobile collection pages — it's the single highest-impact mobile PLP change and requires no design overhaul.

## Quick Headlines

**Checkout Mobile UX — Baymard's 6 Considerations:** [Baymard's dedicated mobile checkout guide](https://baymard.com/blog/mobile-checkout) highlights that mobile-specific checkout issues — keyboard type mismatches (showing alphabetic keyboard for numeric fields), auto-fill not triggering, and input fields obscured by the keyboard — account for a significant share of mobile checkout abandonment that desktop audits miss. These are distinct from desktop checkout friction and require a separate mobile-only audit pass. Running checkout on a real device (not emulator) once per quarter catches regressions that desktop testing never surfaces.

## Action Items
- [ ] Count live checkout form fields — if over 15, identify and remove duplicate address fields, optional fields, and any field answerable by default (e.g., country pre-filled by geo-IP)
- [ ] Confirm guest checkout is the **primary and largest CTA** on the account/checkout entry screen — if "Sign In" is more prominent than "Guest Checkout," flip it today
- [ ] Add Shop Pay / Apple Pay / Google Pay express checkout if not live — benchmark a **+20%+ mobile CVR lift** as the expected return
- [ ] Move your checkout trust badge inline with the payment form — test McAfee Secure or SSL seal placement directly above or beside the card number field
- [ ] Audit top 3 collection pages: add all 5 essential filter types (price, rating, brand, size, category-specific) and confirm "Best Sellers" is the default sort
- [ ] Instrument filter usage in GA4 on collection pages — track filter_apply events with filter type and value; run for 2 weeks before redesigning the filter hierarchy

## Executive Summary

Alani Nu is a health-and-wellness brand built for young, active women who refuse to compromise — on flavor, on quality, or on showing up for themselves. Founded in 2017 by fitness entrepreneur and influencer Katy Hearn in Louisville, Kentucky, Alani Nu was born from a simple frustration: the supplement market wasn't honestly serving women. So Katy built a brand that did.

The brand voice is warm, confident, and unapologetically feminine — it talks to women like a knowledgeable best friend who happens to know a lot about nutrition. It doesn't condescend. It doesn't over-explain. It empowers. The writing feels high-energy without being frantic, clean without being clinical, and fun without being frivolous.

Alani Nu's core personality lives at the intersection of credibility and approachability. Every product claim is grounded in transparent ingredient lists and clean formulas. Every piece of communication celebrates the customer — her goals, her hustle, her wins. Acquired by Celsius Holdings in early 2025 at a $1.8B valuation, the brand's voice remains rooted in its founding community and should preserve that authentic, woman-first identity.

### Voice Attributes Detail

#### Attribute 1: Empowering
- **What it means**: Every interaction should leave the customer feeling capable and motivated — not lectured or sold to. The brand exists to support her, not define her.
- **How it shows up**: Second-person copy that centers the customer ("your every move," "your goals," "beside you"). Action-oriented language. Celebrating progress over perfection.
- **What to avoid**: Prescriptive language ("you should"), body-shaming adjacency, anything that implies the customer is lacking.
- **Evidence**: "To help you succeed — whether you want to hit your last rep, hold your next handstand, or balance your hormones, we want to be beside you." (About Alani Nu page)
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 2: Unapologetically Bold
- **What it means**: The brand owns its femininity and fun-forward identity without apology. This extends to flavors (Pink Slush, Mimosa, Cosmic Stardust), packaging, and copy.
- **How it shows up**: Phrases like "unapologetically bold," flavor-forward language, confidence in product claims, lack of defensive qualifications.
- **What to avoid**: Toning down the fun to seem "serious," over-qualifying product descriptions, using masculine energy drink tropes.
- **Evidence**: "Just like you, our flavors are unapologetically bold." "Enjoy the unapologetically bold and refreshing Pink Slush flavor." (Product descriptions via retailer listings)
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 3: Clean & Transparent
- **What it means**: Ingredient transparency is a core brand promise, not just a marketing claim. The brand specifically calls out what's *not* in products as much as what is.
- **How it shows up**: "Without the fillers," "nothing you don't need," "clean formula," "transparent label," "strategically designed to provide everything you need and nothing you don't."
- **What to avoid**: Vague benefit claims without substance, listing ingredients without context, health washing.
- **Evidence**: "Strategically designed to provide users with everything they need and nothing they don't." "Clean formulations that women could confidently incorporate into their fitness journeys." (About page and marketing analysis)
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 4: Fun & Playful

- **How it shows up**: Playful flavor names (Cherry Slush, Dream Float, Sherbet Swirl), casual social content, UGC-driven marketing, exclamation points and conversational phrasing.
- **What to avoid**: Taking the fun so far it undermines credibility, humor at the expense of transparency, using trendy language that dates quickly.
- **Evidence**: Brand described as "vibrant health-and-wellness brand." TikTok content uses popular sounds with product content. Flavor names are nostalgic/playful. (Multiple sources)
- **Confidence**: Medium — inferred from product naming and social strategy; no explicit tone documentation found.

#### Attribute 5: Honest & Direct

- **How it shows up**: Concrete claims ("200mg caffeine," "zero sugar," "10 calories"), the willingness to say "without the fillers or BS," direct calls to action.

- **Evidence**: "Gimmick-free supplements." "Eliminate all the negative ingredients and fillers." "Everything you need, nothing you don't." (About page, product descriptions)
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 6: Community-Centered

- **How it shows up**: UGC integration, collegiate ambassador program, social giveaways that require engagement, influencer partnerships rooted in alignment over reach.

## Messaging Framework (Confidence: Medium)

### Primary Value Proposition
Alani Nu gives women the clean, delicious, trustworthy supplements they deserve — so nothing gets in the way of showing up fully.

Variations observed:
- "Empowering your every move." (Tagline — About page)
- "Wellness should feel good and taste good." (Website messaging)
- "Clean, effective products that support your goals — without the fillers or BS." (Product positioning)
- "A vibrant health-and-wellness brand focused on empowering your every move." (Brand description)

### Key Message Pillars

1. **Clean Formulas You Can Trust**
   - Core idea: Every product is transparent, purposeful, and free of unnecessary additives.
   - When to use: Product descriptions, ingredient callouts, credibility-building content, retail shelf copy.
   - Example phrasing: "Everything you need. Nothing you don't." / "Clean formula. Transparent label. No guessing."

2. **Made for Women, By Someone Who Gets It**
   - Core idea: Alani Nu was built from a real gap in the market — Katy Hearn couldn't recommend other brands to the women she worked with, so she made one herself.
   - When to use: Brand story content, About Us, origin storytelling, founder-driven campaigns.
   - Example phrasing: "Built by a woman who refused to settle for supplements that weren't honest." / "She couldn't find a brand she'd stand behind — so she built it."

3. **Bold Flavor, Real Results**
   - Core idea: You don't have to choose between performance and taste. Alani Nu delivers both.
   - When to use: Energy drink campaigns, product launches, social media, retail.
   - Example phrasing: "Unapologetically bold flavors. Zero sugar. Real energy." / "Guilt-free and crash-free — every single sip."

4. **Your Goals, Supported**
   - Core idea: Whatever "winning" looks like for you today, Alani Nu is beside you.
   - When to use: Campaign headlines, lifestyle content, ambassador communications, community content.
   - Example phrasing: "Whether it's your last rep or your first handstand — we're here." / "Show up for yourself. We've got the rest."

### Competitive Positioning
- **vs. Monster / Red Bull**: Alani Nu speaks directly to women while traditional energy brands speak past them. The formula is cleaner, the flavors are more inventive, and the community is built on shared goals — not just caffeine.
- **vs. Celsius**: Where Celsius is science-forward and performance-focused, Alani Nu is community-forward and lifestyle-focused. Fun and credibility coexist. Flavor is a feature, not an afterthought.
- **vs. Generic Supplement Brands**: Most supplement brands confuse or intimidate. Alani Nu simplifies — clean labels, honest ingredients, no proprietary blends hiding behind jargon.
- **vs. Status Quo (doing nothing)**: Women deserve products designed specifically for them — not adapted from male-focused formulas.

### Context-Specific Guidelines

#### Social Media (Instagram / TikTok)
- **Overall tone**: Best friend energy — fast, fun, shareable, real.
- **Opening approach**: Dive straight in. Hook in the first word or frame.
- **Do's**: Use UGC, feature real customers, lean into flavor launches, capitalize on trends selectively, use humor rooted in shared experience.
- **Don'ts**: Over-produce (authenticity is the asset), use corporate language, post without visual punch, ignore comments.
- **Example**: "Pink Slush just dropped. You're welcome. 🩷 Zero sugar. 200mg caffeine. 15 calories. Don't @ us."

#### Product Descriptions
- **Overall tone**: Energetic, clear, and confident — like a knowledgeable friend explaining why you'll love this.
- **Opening approach**: Lead with flavor and feeling, follow with function.
- **Do's**: Name the caffeine content, call out zero sugar, use sensory language for flavors, mention what's NOT in the formula.
- **Don'ts**: Use passive voice, bury the benefit, use unsubstantiated superlatives.
- **Example**: "Mimosa flavor hits like your favorite brunch — without the next-day regret. 200mg caffeine, zero sugar, 10 calories. Crack one. You've earned it."

#### Email / CRM
- **Overall tone**: Warm, personal, and value-first — not transactional.
- **Opening approach**: Address the reader directly. Acknowledge the moment (launch, season, milestone).
- **Do's**: Use the customer's name, tie back to her goals, announce flavor launches with excitement, reward loyalty genuinely.
- **Don'ts**: Lead with discount codes (they devalue the brand), send generic blasts, use pushy urgency tactics.
- **Example subject line**: "Your next obsession just dropped 🌟" / "We built this one for you."

#### About / Brand Story
- **Overall tone**: Genuine, conversational, mission-driven — not corporate.
- **Opening approach**: Start with the founder's real frustration, not accolades.
- **Do's**: Credit Katy Hearn's origins, reference the community of women that shaped the brand, express humility alongside confidence.
- **Don'ts**: Oversell the acquisition story, use corporate boilerplate, lose the human voice.

| Term | Reason |
|------|--------|

| "Secret formula" | Undermines trust and clean label positioning |

## Language to Avoid (Confidence: Medium)

### Anti-Patterns
1. **"Clinically proven to..."** — Problem: Unsubstantiated without specific citation; erodes trust. Better: "Formulated with [X]mg of [ingredient] to support [benefit]."
2. **Passive product-first copy** (e.g., "This product contains...") — Problem: Cold and distancing. Better: Center the customer ("You get 200mg of caffeine and zero sugar...")
3. **Hedging language** ("may help," "could support") — Problem: Weakens conviction and brand confidence. Better: Use concrete claims where substantiated, or reframe as philosophy ("We believe in formulas that actually do something.")
4. **Generic wellness buzzwords** ("balance," "wellness journey," "holistic") without specificity — Problem: Vague and indistinguishable from hundreds of other brands. Better: Ground claims in real ingredients or real scenarios.
5. **Overly formal sign-offs** ("Best regards," "Sincerely") in customer-facing copy — Problem: Kills the brand voice. Better: "You've got this." / "See you at the gym." / "— The Alani Nu Team"
